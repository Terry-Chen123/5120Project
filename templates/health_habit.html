    Health Habit

    Maintain good health habits is the essential part to protect your child away from pneumonia disease.

    Hand Washing

    Hand Washing


    Washing hands prevents illnesses and spread of infections to others. Frequent hand washing with warm water and soap is very important to prevent viruses or bacteria from entering the body when hands come in contact with your child’s nose or mouth.


    Fun facts about hand washing

    Figure - How often, if ever, do you wash your hands with soap?

    • About four in ten Australians don’t wash their hands with soap after they go to the bathroom at home.
    • Two out of five people in the world do not have a hand washing facility with soap and water on premises.
    • Almost half of the schools in the world don’t have hand washing facilities with soap and water for students.


    Hand Washing Steps

    Steps For Hand Washing

    1. Wet your hands with warm water (not too hot or cold).
    2. Use enough liquid or bar soap to cover your hand.
    3. Rub fronts and backs of hands and in between fingers.
        Hum or sing "Twinkle, twinkle, Little Star" while you
        wash to remind you to scrub for 20 seconds.
    4. Rinse your hands well under the running water.
    5. Dry with a clean towel or air dryer. If possible, use
        your paper towel to turn off the sink.

    Enough Sleep

    Sufficient Sleep


    Enough sleep is essential for growth, immunity, learning and memory and is important for helping a child heal and recover. Healthy sleep means a good quantity and quality of sleep, with regular sleep routines.


    How much sleep does a child need?



    • Around 4 in 5 children thought they were getting enough sleep, when in fact they don't meet minimum sleep guidelines.
    • Nearly all 6-7 years age children were getting the required minimum hours of sleep on school days, but only half of them were.
    • Children aged 12-17 years were less likely to get the required minimum hours of sleep on school nights than non-school nights.


    Tips To Help Children Sleep Well

    Make sure your child goes to bed early enough to get the sleep they need. Once you have set an appropriate bed time, stick to it — even at the weekend.

    Follow the same routine every day: bath or shower, change into pyjamas, brush teeth, read or spend quiet time in their bedroom, lights out and go to sleep.

    Busy children need some time to relax. Consider playing soft music or reading to them.

    Ensure the bedroom is dark and quiet. If your child is anxious or afraid at night, use a night light.

    Make sure your child avoids tea and chocolate, especially in the afternoon or at the night.

    Turning off computers, tablets and television 1 hour before bedtime should help your child sleep better.


    Physical Activity

    Physical Activity


    Physical activity is any activity that involves moving your body. It includes everyday activities, physically active play, and organised sports and exercise. Regular physical activity has significant benefits for your child, giving them a strong body to fight diseases.


    General data about Physical Activity



    • Around 35% of Australian children aged 0-14 prefer swimming.
    • Less than one-quarter (23%) of children aged 5-14 undertook the recommended 60 minutes of physical activity every day.
    • On average, children aged 5-14 spent more than 2 hours each day sitting or lying down for screen-based activities. Children aged 2-4 spent 83 minutes a day on average in front of screens.
    • Two-third (67%) of children aged 0-14 participated in organised physical activities outside of school hours at least once a fortnight.

    Different Levels Of Physical Activity

    Light physical activity could be going for a stroll, playing a musical instrument or standing up to paint at an easel.

    Moderate physical activity gets children gently huffing and puffing, including walking quickly, riding to Child Care school or a friend’s house, dancing, skipping or jumping in puddles or flying kites as well as swimming.

    Vigorous physical activity gets children huffing and puffing a lot, as well as sweating, including playing running games like ‘keepings off’ or ‘chasey’, riding a bike fast, playing organised sports like soccer, basketball, touch football or netball.

    Energetic play is a natural way for children to move and be active. For example, babies rock and kick their feet. Older children run, jump, twirl, kick, throw, dance to music, play on playground equipment, enjoy rough-and-tumble play, and much more.

    Optimum Time About Being Active



    Age How much each day? Ideas for what to do
    Infants (0 – 12 months) Should be physically active several times daily. At least 30 minutes of tummy time through the day. Active play starts from birth, especially through floor-based activities including tummy-time, reaching, pushing, pulling and crawling.
    Toddlers (1 – 2 years) At least 180 minutes (3 hours) of physical activity throughout the day, including playing outdoors. Recommend to light physical activity, such as standing up, moving around, rolling and playing, as well as more energetic play like skipping, hopping, running and jumping.
    Pre-schoolers (3 – 5 years) At least 180 minutes (3 hours) of physical activity throughout the day, including at least 60 minutes of moderate – vigorous intensity physical activity. Recommend to moderate physical activity and vigorous physical activity, such as dancing, bike riding, kite-flying, swimming and playing organised sports like basketball.
