    • Introduction of pneumonia :

    Pneumonia Symptoms on Children.

    -- has a cough and fever

    -- be short of breath or have difficulty breathing

    -- stomach ache and/or vomit

    -- be irritable or tired & lose appetite

    Is Pneumonia Contagious?

    In general, pneumonia is not contagious, but the upper respiratory viruses and bacteria that lead to it are.
    When these germs are in fluid from the mouth or nose of someone who's infected, that person can spread the illness through coughs and sneezes. While many cases of pneumonia can be mild, left untreated some cases of pneumonia can be serious and even life-threatening. Sharing cups and utensils, touching the used tissues or handkerchiefs of an infected person also can spread pneumonia. So, it's best to keep kids away from anyone with symptoms of a respiratory infection.

    • Prevention of Pneumonia :

    Prevenar 13 - covers 13 strains of pneumococcal disease;
    Pneumovax 23 - covers 23 strains of pneumococcal disease.

    Pneumococcal vaccines are provided free under the National Immunisation Program (NIP) for children who are eligible: All children at 2, 4 and 12 months of age. An extra dose at 6 months for Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander children who live in Queensland, Northern Territory, Western Australia or South Australia. Children of all ages who have certain medical conditions that put them at higher risk of getting serious pneumococcal disease

    Simple precautions that children and family can do to prevent the spread of the viruses and bacteria to reduce the chances of getting pneumonia:

    • Make sure you regularly wash hands with warm, soapy water.

    • Keep your hands away from your face as much as possible.

    • Get enough rest and do moderate exercise.

    • Cough or sneeze into a tissue or your elbows.

    • Treatment of Pneumonia in Children

    -- Hospital admission. Take your child to the GP as soon as possible.

    -- Make that your child gets as much rest as possible.

    -- Cover the mouth with tissue or elbows when sneezing or coughing.

    -- Keep your home smoke free. Infants and young children 2 are more likely to get pneumonia. Smoke can make pneumonia worse.

    -- Don’t worry if your child has a fever and isn’t hungry. Your child’s appetite will improve as your child starts to feel better.

    -- Don’t get the pneumonia vaccine and the flu vaccine at the same time, this may increase the risk of children having fever-related seizures.

    -- It is important to give your child fluids frequently to prevent dehydration. Offer small sips of water, and offer babies breastmilk or formula more often.
